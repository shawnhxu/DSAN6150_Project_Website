Survival Analysis Conclusions

Demographic Data

To determine the demographic factors that have an association with type II diabetes diagnosis we looked at several variables in the NHANES demographic data file. The demographics data file had 8 demographic variables of the 29 columns in the dataframe. Of these 8, we chose to exclude age, marital status, and pregnancy status. We excluded marital status and pregnancy status because they do not necessarily remain the same before and after a hypothetical diabetes development. The remaining variables that we did examine were gender, race, country of birth, education level, and family income level.

We generated Kaplan-Meier survival curves for each of these variables with the event being diabetes diagnosis and the time to event being the age at which the subject was diagnosed. Of the five variables, we found a significant difference in survival for race, gender, and country of birth at the p = 0.05 significance level. There was no significant difference in survival based on either of the two socioeconomic factors we analyzed: education level and family income.

Dietary Data

Of the Dietary variables explored from NHANES, only a hand yielded survival p values that would suggest a significant relationship. Both the count of meals from fast food or pizza places as well as the dietary health index yielded p values of less than 0.001. Interestingly, the count of meals not prepared at home also had a traditionally significant p value of 0.026. It is therefore extremely unlikely that the patterns in the data would be observed if there was not a relationship between these variables and the likeliness and timing of developing diabetes. This suggests that there might be some relationship between the frequency of eating out, notably at fast food or pizza places, dietary health, and diabetes diagnoses. These three variables where therefore selected for the investigation of syndemics.

Laboratory Data

In an attempt to associate physiological measurements to the development of diabetes, we decided to include the NHANES Laboratory data set which was a part of the main NHANES data source. The Laboratory Dataset included more than 40 different physiological tests which resulted in 13,000+ rows and 338 features.

To identify key features influencing diabetes development, we fitted Cox Proportional Hazards models for each feature in the merged laboratory dataset. Statistically significant features were selected based on p-values and hazard ratios. Kaplan-Meier survival curves were then generated, with annotated p-values highlighting relationships between physiological features and survival time.

Among the analyzed features, only LBXVBZN (Blood Benzonitrile, ng/mL) showed a statistically significant difference in survival analysis across benzonitrile ranges. While its role in diabetes is not well-studied, benzonitrile is naturally found in foods like cheese, cocoa, fish, and honey. The NCBI National Library of Medicine identifies it as harmful upon ingestion or skin contact, with potential negative effects on metabolic processes in both humans and experimental animals.

Questionnaire Data

The questionnaire data section of the NHANES data set provided an overview of import social, environmental, and external health factors that could impact a diabetes diagnosis. Over the almost 40 variables in the questionnaire data set, we decided to focus on five: alcohol consumption, depression, access to health care, insurance coverage, and occupation. These variables are prominent in many cultures and/or were mentioned in our review of the literature which is why we felt they were important to explore. As mentioned in other parts of the questionnaire analysis, each of these variables is an individual data set with multiple different variables. Some of the variables are quite specific, so we selected the few variables that are most representative or are the best at summarising that variable.

For each analysis, similarly to the laboratory data, we fitted Cox Proportional Hazards models for each features and then created Kaplan-Meier curves to show how survival probability changes over time. We found five significant variables: average daily alcohol consumption over the past twelve months, insurance coverage, general health condition, employment status, and hours worked over the past week.

Syndemic Analysis

Once each individual analysis was done, our next step was combining the variables to see how the influence each other. There were twelve significant variables. As this number would produce a very large amount of combinations, we decided to analyse the variables with the highest significance (i.e. those with p < 0.001). We ended up analysing six variables. To analyse these combinations, we wrote a function to loop through every combination of the variables and fit each one to a Cox Proportional Hazards model with an interaction term. The variable combination and the p value of the interaction term were recorded in a data frame.

There were 12 significant combinations. Every combination mentioned race and either diet or alcohol consumption. Eleven out of the twelve mentioned diet healthiness. This confirms that diabetes is a disease heavily affected by food and alcohol consumption that a healthy diet and lower alcohol consumption may be key to managing the condition.

Extra Steps

There are many directions that future work on this project can go in. The most important next step that we see is to analyze the statistically significant variables in conjunction with any of the non-statistically significant variables that we found to see if these variables have any influence. This would tell us more about how influential such variables are, and provide more information about what other syndemics may exist that our current analysis is overlooking.
